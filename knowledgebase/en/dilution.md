# Dilution

Sources:
- SEC Investor.gov: https://www.investor.gov
- Company filings on SEC EDGAR: https://www.sec.gov/search-filings

Dilution happens when a company issues new shares or securities that can become shares, reducing existing shareholders' ownership percentage.

Common causes:
- Secondary stock offerings.
- Convertible notes.
- Warrants.
- Stock-based compensation.
- Mergers paid with shares.

Economic interpretation:
- Dilution is not always negative. If new capital funds profitable growth, long-term value may still increase.
- It becomes a bigger concern when shares are issued at low prices, when cash burn is high, or when the business does not generate returns above the cost of capital.
- Fully diluted share count matters more than basic share count for high-growth companies with many convertible instruments.

When explaining news:
- Separate "cash raised today" from "possible future dilution".
- Look for conversion price, strike price, maturity and total potential shares.
- Explain that the stock may fall if investors focus on dilution, even if the company improves liquidity.
